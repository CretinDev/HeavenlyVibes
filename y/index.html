Golden Hour, Lake Nipissing

What an evening of candid portraiture looks like with me

We meet up as the light starts turning gold, usually along the sandbars of Lake Nipissing where the water goes shallow and calm and the whole sky opens up behind you. There's no studio, no backdrop, no stiff posing — just you, the lake, and that hour before sunset where everything looks like it's lit from within.

I shoot candid. That means we walk, we talk, you wade out into the water or sit on the sand with the people you came with, and I move around you catching the moments as they actually happen — a laugh, a glance, someone getting splashed. If you've never been photographed like this before, it takes about five minutes to stop noticing the camera entirely.

Everything is shot on 35mm film with a Pentax K1000, the same camera that's been making portraits look like memories for fifty years. Film forces a slower pace — I'm not machine-gunning a thousand frames hoping one works, I'm watching for the actual moment. That's part of why the photos come out feeling timeless instead of like a phone snapshot.

Book an appointment session and you'll also walk away with a complimentary stereoscopic 3D photo, shot on a Nimslo 3D — a strange, wonderful little camera from the 80s that captures genuine depth. It's the kind of keepsake nobody else is going to hand you.

After we wrap, the film gets developed and scanned, and I do basic color-accurate grading before anything gets delivered. No rush, no pressure to perform — just an evening by the water that happens to leave you with portraits worth keeping.

— Jim Craven
